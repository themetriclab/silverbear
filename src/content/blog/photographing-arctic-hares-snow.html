Understanding Arctic Hares in Their Snowy Habitat

Arctic Hare Adaptations to Cold and Snow

  • White winter coat: Arctic hares turn nearly pure white with black ear tips to blend seamlessly in snow.
  • Compact body and small ears: Minimizes heat loss and exposure to Arctic winds.
  • Fur-covered feet: Help them run on snow and ice without sinking and keep feet warm.
  • Sheltering habits: Use snow dens or scrapes to shelter from wind and predators.
  • Speed and agility: Can sprint up to 60 km/h, dodging predators with quick turns and long bounds.

Behavior Patterns to Expect

  • Mostly crepuscular, active at dawn and dusk; may rest or forage slowly during daylight, especially in winter.
  • Hares often curl into tight balls in snow scrapes—watch for subtle behaviors like yawning or grooming for engaging shots.
  • Patience is critical to capture natural movements and expressions.

Churchill’s Arctic Conditions

  • Temperatures range from –20°C to –30°C, with wind chills lower.
  • Expect harsh wind, flat light, and low contrast conditions.
  • Your guide will help plan shoots around weather and lighting.

Key Challenges in Photographing Arctic Hares in Snow

Low Contrast Makes Focus and Exposure Hard

  • White fur blends into white snow, causing autofocus systems to lose the subject.
  • Flat lighting (overcast skies) erases shadows that define shape and texture.
  • The hare can disappear visually, except for eyes and black ear tips.

Exposure Pitfalls

  • Camera meters underexpose scenes with lots of bright snow, making hares look gray.
  • Overexposure risks blowing out detail and losing fur texture.
  • Fast-moving hares require very fast shutter speeds; slower speeds mean motion blur.

Terrain Limits Your Angles

  • Rocks, shrubs, and snow drifts provide hides for hares but restrict your approach and shooting positions.
  • Be ready to reposition or wait for the hare to move for optimal framing.

Arctic Wildlife Exposure Tips for Snow Photography

Master Exposure Compensation

Dial in +1 to +2 EV exposure compensation in aperture- or shutter-priority modes. This brightens snow and fur from dull gray to crisp white.

Use Manual Exposure When Possible

  • Lock exposure on a mid-tone reference, like rocks or veldt, to avoid fluctuations.
  • Avoid letting the camera meter re-adjust as you reframe.

Histogram and Spot Metering

  • Check histogram frequently: snow should be bright but avoid clipping highlights.
  • Spot meter on the hare’s fur, then add +1 to +2 stops exposure compensation.

Composition Ideas for Snow Wildlife Photography

Use Light and Texture to Define Form

  • Favor side or low-angle light to create shadows in snow and show fur texture.
  • Avoid mid-day flat light which flattens the scene.

Incorporate Environmental Elements

  • Frame hares with rocks, bushes, and drifts to add scale and interest.
  • Use foreground or background elements slightly out-of-focus for depth.

Apply Rule of Thirds and Space for Movement

  • Position the hare off-center, leaving space and direction in the frame.
  • When hares are running, leave “run-into” space in front.

Emphasize Negative Space

  • Use vast snowy negatives to highlight the hare’s Arctic isolation.

Ground-Level Arctic Hare Photography: Intimacy and Impact

Why Shoot at Eye Level?

  • Creates a connection between viewer and animal.
  • Simplifies busy backgrounds.
  • Shows scale and presence of the hare.

Ethical Approaches to Getting Close

  • Move slowly and indirectly; never rush.
  • Crawl the last few meters to avoid chasing or startling hares.
  • Watch for stress signals — upright, tense hares mean stop or back off.
  • Avoid blocking escape routes.

  • Bean bags or low-profile ground pods for stability.
  • Kneepads and waterproof, insulated clothing (Patagonia, Arc’teryx, Sitka).
  • Layered gloves and hand warmers to endure cold and keep steady.
